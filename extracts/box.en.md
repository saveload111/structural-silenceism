# The Box

There was a box against the north wall of the living room. Ninety-two centimeters long, fifty-six wide, forty-two high. Nanmu wood, the lacquer worn white at the edges. Every year after the rainy season, my mother wiped it with a dry cloth. My father never let anyone sit on it, never let it be pushed too close to the wall.

It had been in the family at least four generations. When I was small, I drew on its surface with crayons; the next day the marks were always gone. My grandmother once lifted me onto it and said it was higher than a stool. Later I moved out, and every time I came back, it was in the same place.

He does not say the direction. It is not that he cannot say it. It is that he does not.

Last year we moved. A neighbor asked what it was. My father said: "Old furniture." When the truck came, I asked what was inside. He said: "Nothing." But he did not open it in front of the neighbor. An empty box took four people to carry onto the truck. After we settled in, my mother put a few rolls of old quilts inside, then took them out again, saying the position was wrong.

An empty space requires more force than a full one.

Now it sits in the storage room of the new house. The door is usually closed. Sometimes I pass by and my steps slow down without reason. I have never opened it. In this house, no one has ever brought up the need to open it.

**Colophon**: The contents of this box lie outside the boundary of the above narrative. The narrative confirms only two facts: that the box is empty, and that emptiness requires force. What is inside the box is not hidden. It is positioned as unopenable.
