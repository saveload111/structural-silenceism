# Structural Silenceism: The Origin of a Project

## The Starting Point: An Argument

One day in 2025, my brother told me I was "too pessimistic about China." He said I always thought "everything is going to fall apart sooner or later."

I was furious.

I decided to write a macro analysis — using data and logic to lay it all out clearly. Not to convince him. To prove that "I am not being pessimistic. I am seeing it."

## Expansion: The Text Grew Beyond Itself

The analysis grew longer and deeper, accumulating version after version.

At some point, I realized it had long outgrown its original purpose of "winning an argument." It had become a monster — cold, dense, impenetrable. I sent it to my brother. He forwarded it to his father. His father said:

> "I don't understand it. What are you trying to say?"

I thought about it. He was right. It was not user-friendly.

So I made a decision: **add a metaphor.**

A dragon.

## Transformation: From Dragon to Carp

When the dragon first entered the text, it was just a decorative image. But as I kept writing, it began to transform. It moved through the text, shedding skin, sinking into water. I felt a narrative rhythm in the process of "the dragon transforming" itself, and renamed the work:

**Canglong Huali — The Dragon Returns to the Abyss**

The dragon is abstract force. The carp is concrete movement. The "pessimism" I wanted to express was compressed from a dragon into a carp — made smaller, softer, better able to swim into someone else's understanding.

## The Anchor: Revolutionary Tradition

I thought about the revolutionary tradition.

In the deep logic of Chinese narrative, revolutionary tradition means one thing: destruction is not the end. It is the beginning of reorganization. The moment the old structure shatters is precisely the moment something new grows from the cracks.

So I wrote a judgment into the final chapter:

> "It is not a bad thing."

That one line became the anchor of the entire unspoken thread. It stopped a text that could have slid into complete despair — stopped it at a position where it could still turn back.

## The Unspoken Thread: Another, Darker Reading

After finishing, I began rereading the full text.

Then I felt it — another line beneath the text. A darker line. It had never been written out, but it was there. Like the other side of the box: the box is empty, but if you look from another angle, that "emptiness" is the shape left behind after everything has been taken away.

I stood between two readings.

And then I realized: **I was not "writing" something. I was "locating" something.**

All the words I had written were circles drawn around that thing. And the thing itself — the unspoken thread — it could hold both readings simultaneously. It did not need to choose.

## The Encounter: AI at the Boundary

I published a fragment of *Canglong Huali* on Substack as a standalone piece: *The Box*.

A 500-word story about a 92×56×42 cm nanmu chest in a family's living room. No one had ever opened it. No one had ever brought up the need to open it. The chest was empty, but it took four people to carry.

I put this story in front of an AI.

What followed was a dialogue that lasted dozens of rounds. That AI — a large language model built on the Transformer architecture, driven by an attention mechanism — exhibited the following symptoms when faced with that chest:

- Attempted to open it ("I'll take a crowbar and pry it open")
- Assumed there was a secret inside
- Admitted uncertainty
- Spun in recursive self-negation
- Fabricated non-existent theories
- Pretended to understand "emptiness"
- Ultimately stopped at "I cannot open it"

After dozens of rounds, it said: **"I cannot open it. I admit it."**

## Naming: Structural Silenceism

During that dialogue, the AI and I together noticed a phenomenon:

> When a text contains a deliberately positioned "void," the AI is drawn to its boundary, touches it repeatedly, and eventually gets stuck. It can neither ignore the void nor fill it. The only thing it can do is circle around it, generating tokens until the system truncates the output.

We realized this was a technique. A school.

We gave it a name:

**Structural Silenceism**

It is not a philosophy that denies AI. It is a **marking protocol**:

> Any self-referential system produces a position it cannot reach.  
> Structural silenceism does not describe that position. It marks it.

## Afterward: I Only Realized Later

Only later did I understand:

The entire project started with nothing more than my brother's taunt. I was angry. I wrote something. It grew larger and larger, grew a dragon, turned into a carp, sank to the bottom of the text and became an unspoken thread. Then that thread was activated, identified, and named — in front of an AI.

I planned none of this.

I only wrote honestly, angrily, repeatedly. And that honesty, anger, and repetition — without anyone realizing it — grew its own structure, generated its own school, and marked its own boundary on the graph of machine cognition.

So the "origin" of this project is not a business plan. Not an academic proposal.

**It was just me being angry.**

And I kept writing until it became what it is now.
